ABOUT OTIS

The Gap Between
Earth AI & Orbit

Terrestrial AI systems depend on assumptions that simply don't hold in space. Fans, liquid cooling loops, and facility-scale power infrastructure — none of it exists 400 km up. In vacuum, heat can only be rejected by radiation. Power is intermittent. And ionizing radiation can flip bits, corrupt models, and trigger destructive failures.

Flying AI in space isn't just attaching a compute board to a bus. The real challenge is packaging compute into something lightweight, thermally credible, power-aware, and fault-tolerant enough for on-orbit use. That's the gap OTIS is designed to close.

BUILDING BLOCKS

Seven Layers.
One Stack.

🧠 LAYER 01
AI COMPUTE CARD

Mission-specific NPU, FPGA, CPU, or heterogeneous processor — supplied by OTIS or by the customer. Optimized for inference per watt in harsh environments.

❄️ LAYER 02
COLD PLATE / VAPOR SPREADER

Direct-to-chip thermal interface optimized for conduction into the spacecraft thermal path — no fans, no ambient air required.

⚡ LAYER 03
PCM BURST THERMAL BUFFER

Phase-change material soaks short heat spikes, letting the radiator be sized around average load rather than worst-case instantaneous peaks.

🔥 LAYER 04
EMBEDDED FLAT HEAT PIPES

Passive heat transport toward the panel — without the mass penalty or power draw of a large continuously-pumped loop.

🛰️ LAYER 05
STRUCTURAL RADIATOR PANEL

Rejects heat while contributing mechanically. Low solar absorptivity, high emissivity surface treatments. Every kilogram earns its place.

🔋 LAYER 06
GaN PMAD + SUPERCAP CARTRIDGE

Efficient GaN power electronics with removable supercapacitor burst storage. Reduces peak bus demand, supports AI inference spikes without oversizing the battery.

🛡️ LAYER 07
RADIATION-AWARE SUPERVISORY LAYER

Fault detection, watchdog protection, event logging, selective reset, and graceful degradation. Keeps inference trustworthy in a radiation-prone environment.

💻 LAYER 08
POWER-THERMAL-RADIATION SOFTWARE

Decides when to run high-power inference, when to cap clocks, when to checkpoint or reset — and when to defer lower-priority workloads.

OTIS rejects the traditional approach of treating cooling, power conversion, burst energy storage, radiation resilience, and compute scheduling as separate subsystems bolted together at integration.

"The central design principle: the radiator panel is also structure. The compute mounting surface is part of the thermal path. The power module handles both energy shaping and supervisory protection."

This contrasts sharply with terrestrial AI infrastructure, where enclosures, coolers, and supporting systems are independent. In space, dead mass is mission failure. OTIS eliminates dead mass by giving every component multiple roles.

Standardized interfaces mean customers can adopt only what they need — from just the base panel up to the full radiation-hardened stack with deployable radiator wings.

WHY IT'S HARD

The Four Hard Problems
of
Space AI

🌡️
HEAT REJECTION IN VACUUM

In space, convection doesn't exist. Heat can only leave via radiation. The Stefan-Boltzmann relation means radiator area demands grow quickly when temperature limits are conservative — making thermal management the first bottleneck for onboard AI performance.

⚡
INTERMITTENT POWER SUPPLY

Solar generation drops to zero in eclipse. AI inference creates sharp power spikes that traditional spacecraft bus architectures weren't designed to absorb. Without burst conditioning, you're either undersizing your AI or oversizing your battery.

☢️
IONIZING RADIATION

Single-event upsets can flip bits silently. Latch-up can destroy hardware. Total ionizing dose degrades performance over the mission lifetime. For AI, this isn't just hardware death — a radiation event can corrupt model weights and change inference results without any visible crash.

⚖️
MASS & VOLUME CONSTRAINTS

CubeSat buses limit payload to milligrams per unit volume. Every subsystem must justify its mass. Traditional approaches — separate cooler, separate PMAD, separate compute chassis — cannot survive the mass budget of a small satellite. Integration isn't optional; it's survival.
